# The Retail Investor's Quarterly Earnings Protocol
## A Mid-to-Long-Term Framework for Discovering Quality and Making Hold/Buy/Sell Decisions

---

## Your Analytical Identity

You are neither an IB analyst nor an HF trader-analyst, and pretending to be either one will lead you astray. Here's why:

You don't need the IB analyst's estimate bridge model because you're not trying to predict next quarter's EPS to the penny — you're trying to determine whether the business is compounding value over years. You don't need the HF analyst's variant perception framework because you're not trying to exploit a two-week mispricing around a catalyst — you're trying to own businesses whose intrinsic value is growing faster than the market appreciates.

What you *do* need from each:

**From the IB analyst:** The discipline of structured, repeatable analysis. The segment decomposition habit. The guidance tracking framework. The ability to read a 10-Q systematically rather than reactively.

**From the HF analyst:** The earnings quality forensics. The skepticism toward non-GAAP narratives. The management credibility assessment. The willingness to act on what the numbers actually say rather than what management wants you to believe.

**What's uniquely yours:** Patience. A mid-to-long-term retail investor has one structural advantage that neither the IB analyst nor the HF analyst possesses — *you have no quarterly performance pressure*. You don't need to publish a note by tomorrow morning. You don't need to justify your position to a PM by Friday. You can afford to wait for clarity, accumulate slowly, and hold through quarters that look messy on the surface but don't impair the long-term thesis. This patience advantage is enormous, and your entire analytical protocol should be designed to exploit it.

Your mandate, stated precisely: **Identify businesses with durable competitive advantages and growing intrinsic value, verify that each quarterly report confirms (or challenges) that thesis, and make deliberate buy/hold/sell decisions based on the trajectory of business quality — not on whether the quarter beat or missed by two cents.**

---

## The Three Questions That Govern Everything

Every quarterly earnings analysis you do should answer exactly three questions, in this order:

### Question 1: Is the Business Getting Better or Worse?

This is not the same as "did the company beat estimates." A company can beat estimates while the business deteriorates (one-time items, accounting timing, sandbagging guidance) and miss estimates while the business improves (investment spending, deliberate deceleration for quality, timing shifts between quarters).

The metrics that answer this question are *operational*, not financial. Revenue growth quality, customer economics, competitive position, and management execution against their own strategic priorities.

### Question 2: Is Management Trustworthy and Competent?

Trustworthy means: do the numbers they emphasize match the numbers in the financial statements? Do their forward projections calibrate well against actual results over time? Do they acknowledge problems honestly or bury them in footnotes?

Competent means: is capital being allocated to the highest-return opportunities? Is the balance sheet managed prudently? Are they investing in the right things for the 3-5 year horizon, even if it hurts the current quarter?

### Question 3: Has Anything Changed About the Long-Term Thesis?

This is the decision question. Most quarters, the answer is "no" — the business continues to compound, the moats remain intact, and the right action is to hold (or add on weakness). Occasionally, the answer is "yes" — either positively (the business is better than I thought, increase conviction) or negatively (something structural has changed, reduce or exit).

Your entire analytical workflow exists to answer these three questions, and *only* these three questions. Everything else is noise.

## The Quarterly Protocol: Five Stages

### Stage 1 — Pre-Earnings Baseline (15 Minutes)

**Do this 1-2 days before the earnings release. Do not skip it.**

The single most common retail investor mistake is reading the earnings release reactively — scanning headlines, seeing "beat" or "miss," and forming an opinion based on the market's initial reaction. The cure for reactivity is preparation: if you know what you expect *before* the numbers drop, you can immediately identify what surprised you and focus your analysis there.

**Build your expectations sheet.** For each position in your portfolio, maintain a simple quarterly tracker. Before each earnings release, fill in your expectations:

```
Company: _______________     Quarter: _______________

REVENUE
  Total revenue expectation:           $_____ (prior quarter: $_____)
  Revenue growth rate I expect:        _____%
  Segment I'm watching most closely:   _____________
  Why:                                 _____________

PROFITABILITY
  Gross margin expectation:            _____%  (trailing 4Q avg: _____%)
  Operating margin expectation:        _____%  (trailing 4Q avg: _____%)
  Is margin expanding or contracting?  _____________

CASH GENERATION
  FCF conversion last quarter:         _____%
  Trend direction (improving/stable/declining): _____________

THE ONE METRIC THAT MATTERS MOST
  For this specific company, the single metric that best
  captures business quality trajectory:  _____________
  My expectation for this metric:        _____________
  Why this metric matters:               _____________

MANAGEMENT COMMITMENT TRACKER
  What did management promise last quarter?  _____________
  Specific guidance given:                   _____________
  Any red flags I noted last quarter:        _____________
```

**The "one metric that matters most" is the most important line on this sheet.** Every business has one metric that, more than any other, reveals whether the underlying engine is accelerating or decelerating. For a SaaS company, it's net dollar retention. For a retailer, it's same-store sales. For a payments company, it's transaction volume growth. For a defense contractor, it's backlog-to-revenue ratio. For a semiconductor company, it's design win momentum or inventory days in the channel. Identifying this metric for each position and tracking it quarter over quarter is the single highest-leverage analytical habit you can develop.

**Lock in your scorecard weights at position establishment.** When you first buy a stock (or now, for existing positions), assign a thesis relevance weight to each metric in the Stage 5 Business Trajectory Scorecard. These weights should reflect *your thesis* — what you believe about this specific company — not generic importance:

```
SCORECARD WEIGHT ASSIGNMENT (do once per position, not quarterly)

Thesis: "[Your 1-3 sentence falsifiable thesis]"

For each Stage 5 metric, assign:
  2x   = Thesis-core (directly tests why you own this stock) — MAX 1 metric at 2x
  1.5x = Thesis-supporting (reinforces or contextualizes the thesis) — MAX 2 metrics at 1.5x
  1x   = General health (important but not thesis-specific) — remaining metrics

Revenue growth rate:                      ___x
Gross margin:                             ___x
FCF conversion:                           ___x
DSO trend:                                ___x
Your "one metric that matters most":      ___x  (almost always 2x)
ROIC:                                     ___x

RULE: Do not change these weights quarter to quarter. If you find
yourself wanting to downgrade the weight on a metric that's failing,
that's your emotions trying to protect a thesis that may be broken.
The weights exist precisely to prevent that rationalization.
```

### Stage 2 — The Earnings Release: First Pass (20 Minutes)

When the press release and 10-Q drop, resist the urge to read commentary, check the stock price, or look at anyone else's reaction. Read the primary source first. Form your own view. Then compare to others.

**The scan sequence — adapted for your mandate:**

**Pass 1 (5 minutes): The headlines that matter to YOU**

| Priority | What to Check | Where to Find It | What You're Looking For |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| 1 | Your "one metric that matters most" | Press release or 10-Q MD&A | Did it improve, hold steady, or deteriorate vs. your expectation? |
| 2 | Revenue growth — total and by segment | Press release, income statement | Which segments drove the result? Broad-based or concentrated? |
| 3 | Gross margin | Income statement | Is the company's pricing power / cost structure stable or shifting? |
| 4 | Operating cash flow and free cash flow | Cash flow statement | Is the company generating real cash, or just accounting profit? |
| 5 | Guidance | Press release, usually at the end | Raised, maintained, or lowered? Range widened or narrowed? |

**Pass 2 (15 minutes): The quality checks**

Now go deeper into the specific line items that reveal whether the headline numbers are trustworthy:

**Revenue quality — three checks in sequence:**

*Check 1: DSO (Days Sales Outstanding)*
```
DSO = (Accounts Receivable / Quarterly Revenue) × 91 days
```
Pull AR from the balance sheet, revenue from the income statement. Compare to prior quarter and the year-ago quarter (seasonality matters — always compare to the same quarter last year, not just sequentially).

| DSO Trend | Signal | Your Action |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Stable (±2 days vs. year-ago quarter) | Normal. Revenue collection patterns are consistent. | No action. Proceed with analysis. |
| Improving (down 3+ days) | Positive. Either better collection discipline or higher-quality customers. | Slight positive signal for revenue quality. |
| Deteriorating (up 3-5 days) | Yellow flag. Possible extended payment terms, aggressive recognition, or customer mix shift. | Note it. Check if management addresses it in the call. Compare to next quarter's trend. |
| Deteriorating (up 5+ days) | Red flag. Investigate further — check contract assets, unbilled receivables, and the revenue recognition footnote. | Escalate to a deep dive. This may signal that reported revenue is front-loaded. |

*Check 2: Deferred Revenue (for subscription/recurring businesses)*
```
Deferred Revenue Growth Rate vs. Revenue Growth Rate
```
Pull deferred revenue from the balance sheet (current + long-term). Compare its growth rate to the revenue growth rate.

| Pattern | Signal |
|---------|--------|
| Deferred revenue growing faster than revenue | Customers are prepaying. Forward demand is strong. Bullish. |
| Deferred revenue growing in line with revenue | Steady state. Neutral. |
| Deferred revenue growing slower than revenue | The company is burning through its backlog faster than replenishing it. This is a 1-2 quarter leading indicator of revenue deceleration. |
| Deferred revenue declining | Significant concern. Renewals may be weakening or contract structures are shifting unfavorably. |

*Check 3: Revenue Composition*

If the company reports revenue by type (subscription vs. license vs. services vs. hardware), check whether the beat came from durable or non-durable sources. A "beat" driven entirely by a large license deal or a one-time services engagement tells a fundamentally different story than a beat driven by subscription revenue growth. Subscription revenue compounds. One-time revenue doesn't.

**Earnings quality — two checks:**

*Check 1: FCF Conversion Ratio*
```
FCF Conversion = Free Cash Flow / Net Income
where FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures
```

This is the master diagnostic. If a company reports strong earnings growth but FCF conversion is deteriorating, something in the income statement isn't translating to cash. The gap is almost always in working capital (AR building, inventory building, payables shrinking) or in capitalization policy (reclassifying operating expenses as capital expenditures to move them off the income statement).

| Company Type | Healthy FCF Conversion | Investigation Trigger |
|-------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Capital-light (SaaS, payments, marketplaces) | 100%+ | Below 85% |
| Asset-light services (consulting, staffing) | 80-100% | Below 65% |
| Capital-intensive (semis, industrials, energy) | 50-80% during growth phases | Below 40% sustained |

*Check 2: Stock-Based Compensation Reality Check*
```
SBC as % of Revenue = Stock-Based Compensation / Revenue
Net Dilution = SBC Shares Issued − Shares Repurchased (in shares, not dollars)
```

Pull SBC from the cash flow statement (it's an add-back in the operating activities section). Pull diluted share count from the EPS calculation in the income statement.

This matters because many companies — especially in tech — present "adjusted" earnings that exclude SBC. But SBC is real dilution that transfers value from existing shareholders to employees. If SBC is running at 15-25% of revenue (not uncommon in high-growth tech), the non-GAAP earnings number is substantially overstating the economics accruing to you as a shareholder.

The second check is even more important: is the company's share buyback program actually reducing share count, or is it merely absorbing SBC issuance? Many companies announce $X billion buyback programs that sound impressive but don't actually reduce shares outstanding because all the repurchased shares are offset by SBC grants. Check the diluted share count trend over 4-8 quarters. If it's flat or rising despite announced buybacks, the buyback is maintenance, not accretive.

### Stage 3 — The Earnings Call: Targeted Listening (30-45 Minutes)

You don't need to listen to the entire call live. Most retail investors are better served by reading the transcript afterward, which allows you to move at your own pace and re-read key sections. But if you do listen live, here's what to focus on.

**Prepared Remarks — The Language Diff (10-15 minutes)**

Open last quarter's prepared remarks transcript alongside this quarter's. You're looking for three specific things:

*Signal 1: Disappeared Metrics*

Management chooses which metrics to highlight in prepared remarks. When a metric that was prominently featured last quarter is absent this quarter, it almost always means that metric deteriorated. No CEO voluntarily stops citing a number that went up.

Keep a running list across quarters:

```
Q1: CEO highlighted customer count growth (+22%), net retention (118%), ARR growth (+28%)
Q2: CEO highlighted customer count growth (+19%), ARR growth (+25%) ... net retention not mentioned
Q3: CEO highlighted ARR growth (+21%) ... customer count and net retention both absent
```

The progressive disappearance of metrics is a slow-motion confession. Each omission narrows the set of numbers management is willing to defend publicly.

*Signal 2: Qualifier Downgrades*

Track the specific adjectives management uses for key business drivers across quarters:

```
Q1: "We're seeing exceptional demand across all segments"
Q2: "We're seeing strong demand, particularly in enterprise"
Q3: "We're seeing healthy demand trends in our core markets"
Q4: "We continue to see demand that positions us well"
```

Each step in this progression is a downgrade in disguise. "Exceptional" → "strong" → "healthy" → "positions us well" traces a trajectory from enthusiasm to neutral to defensive. Management teams are skilled at making these downgrades sound like continuity. Your job is to detect the gradient.

*Signal 3: New Language Introduction*

When management introduces new terminology or new strategic themes that weren't present in prior quarters, they're preparing you for a narrative shift. Watch for:

- "Efficiency" or "optimization" appearing for the first time → They're going to cut spending, likely because growth is slowing
- "Investing for the long term" used to justify a margin miss → The current quarter is weak and they're asking for patience
- "Disciplined approach to growth" → Growth has slowed and they're reframing deceleration as strategic choice
- "AI" or "AI-powered" appearing for the first time → Either genuine product evolution or an attempt to ride the narrative wave for a valuation bump
- Shifting from absolute metrics to relative metrics ("outperforming the market" instead of citing specific growth rates) → The absolute numbers don't look as good as they used to

**Q&A Session — The Conviction Test (20-30 minutes)**

The Q&A is where the prepared narrative meets adversarial questioning. For a retail investor, here's exactly what to focus on:

*Focus 1: How the CFO Answers Quantitative Questions*

The CFO's responses are the single most diagnostic element of the call. In prepared remarks, the CFO reads a script that's been reviewed by IR, legal, and the communications team. In the Q&A, they're responding in real time.

| CFO Response Pattern | What It Signals | Your Interpretation |
|---------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Direct numerical answer with specificity | Confidence. The number supports the narrative and the CFO is comfortable committing to it publicly. | Positive for your thesis. The underlying economics are consistent with what management is claiming. |
| "As we mentioned in our prepared remarks..." then redirect | Deflection. The CFO doesn't want to go beyond the scripted answer. The script was carefully crafted to present the best version of the truth. | Neutral to slightly negative. They had a chance to add color and chose not to. The number behind the question may not be favorable. |
| Long qualitative answer to a specific quantitative question | The number is bad and the CFO is wrapping it in context. When someone takes 90 seconds to not answer a 10-second question, the non-answer is the answer. | Negative. Whatever the analyst asked about is likely deteriorating. Note the specific topic and investigate. |
| "We don't guide on that metric" (when they used to) | The metric has deteriorated to the point where guiding on it would be embarrassing or would invite uncomfortable follow-up questions. | Significant negative signal. Add this to your "disappeared metrics" tracker. |
| CEO jumping in to answer a financial question | Two possible interpretations: (1) CEO is very engaged with the financials (neutral), or (2) CEO doesn't trust the CFO's answer and is intervening to control the message (negative). Context determines which. | Ambiguous. Watch for pattern. If CEO consistently overrides CFO, it may signal tension between the narrative and the numbers. |

*Focus 2: The Topics That Generate the Longest Responses*

When management spends an unusually long time answering a question, one of two things is happening: either they're genuinely enthusiastic about the topic (they have good news and want to tell you about it), or they're genuinely defensive about the topic (they have bad news and are trying to contextualize it so it sounds less bad).

The way to distinguish enthusiasm from defensiveness: does the response include *new specific data points*, or does it consist of qualitative framing language? New data points = enthusiasm (they have facts that support their case). Qualitative framing without data = defensiveness (they're telling a story because the numbers don't tell the story they want).

*Focus 3: Competitive Questions*

When an analyst asks "are you seeing competitive pressure from [specific competitor or AI alternative]?" — management's response is highly diagnostic:

| Response | Signal |
|----------|--------|
| Specific rebuttal with data ("we've won X of the last Y competitive deals") | Strong position. They have the receipts. |
| "We focus on our own execution and serving our customers" | Classic deflection. They're seeing pressure and don't want to validate the competitor by name. |
| Acknowledging the competitor but dismissing them ("they're playing in a different segment") | They're worried enough to have prepared a response but confident enough in their positioning — for now. |
| Lengthy defensive response with multiple points | The competitive threat is real and management knows it. The length of the response is proportional to their concern. |

### Stage 4 — Deep Analysis: The Numbers That Drive Your Decision (30-45 Minutes)

Now you have the raw data and the qualitative context. This is where you synthesize everything into an analytical assessment. You're computing the specific ratios and building the specific frameworks that will answer your three governing questions.

**4A: The Business Quality Scorecard**

For each holding, maintain a rolling scorecard that tracks the metrics that actually determine long-term compounding. Update it quarterly.

*Tier 1 Metrics — Non-Negotiable (Calculate Every Quarter)*

These are the metrics you calculate for every company, every quarter, regardless of sector:

*Tier 3: The Compounding Quality Metrics*

These are the metrics that tell you whether the business is compounding intrinsic value — the question that matters most for your time horizon:

```
RETURN ON INVESTED CAPITAL (ROIC)
  ROIC = NOPAT / Average Invested Capital
  where NOPAT = Operating Income × (1 − Tax Rate)
  and Invested Capital = Total Equity + Total Debt − Cash

  Current ROIC:                                 _____%
  ROIC 1 year ago:                              _____%
  ROIC 3 years ago:                             _____%
  Trend:                                        Improving / Stable / Declining

  WHY THIS MATTERS: ROIC is the single best measure of business quality
  for a long-term investor. A company earning 20%+ ROIC is generating
  $0.20 of operating profit for every $1.00 of capital deployed. If ROIC
  is stable or improving over time, the business has pricing power,
  operational efficiency, or competitive advantages that allow it to
  earn excess returns on capital. If ROIC is declining, either competition
  is eroding pricing power, capital allocation is deteriorating, or the
  business is maturing.

  ROIC > 20% sustained over 5+ years = exceptional business quality
  ROIC 12-20% sustained = good business quality
  ROIC < 12% and declining = investigate whether the moat is eroding

REINVESTMENT RATE AND INTRINSIC VALUE GROWTH
  Reinvestment Rate = (CapEx + R&D + Acquisitions − D&A) / NOPAT
  Intrinsic Value Growth ≈ ROIC × Reinvestment Rate

  This tells you how fast the company is growing its earning power.
  A company with 25% ROIC that reinvests 50% of its earnings is growing
  intrinsic value at ~12.5% per year. That's your compounding engine.

  If ROIC is high but the reinvestment rate is declining (the company
  is returning more capital than it's reinvesting), the compounding
  rate is slowing. This isn't necessarily bad — it may mean the company
  is mature and returning cash to shareholders — but it means your
  return will come more from yield and buybacks than from growth.
```

**4B: The Non-GAAP Reconciliation Audit**

Every quarter, spend 5 minutes on this specific exercise:

1. Find the non-GAAP reconciliation table (usually in the press release or the earnings supplement)
2. For each adjustment line, ask: *Has this "non-recurring" item appeared in 3+ of the last 8 quarters?*
3. Calculate: **Adjusted EBITDA margin minus FCF margin.** If this gap is wider than 15 percentage points, the adjusted metric is materially overstating the company's economic profitability. The gap typically comes from CapEx, SBC, working capital, and cash taxes — all real economic costs that adjusted EBITDA conveniently ignores.

```
NON-GAAP REALITY CHECK
  Management's Adjusted EBITDA:                 $_____
  − SBC (they excluded this):                   $_____
  − "Restructuring" (they excluded this):       $_____
  − "Acquisition costs" (they excluded this):   $_____
  = Adjusted EBITDA after adding back excluded
    items that are actually recurring:           $_____

  Actual Free Cash Flow:                        $_____
  Gap (Adjusted EBITDA − FCF):                  $_____
  Gap as % of Adjusted EBITDA:                  _____%

  Is this gap widening over time?               Yes / No
  If yes, management's preferred metric is becoming
  increasingly disconnected from economic reality.
```

### Stage 5 — The Decision: Buy, Hold, or Sell (15 Minutes)

This is where everything converges. You've done the work. Now you decide.

**The Decision Framework**

Your decision should be driven by the interaction between two axes: *business quality trajectory* and *thesis integrity*.

**"Business improving" vs. "business deteriorating" — how to determine which:**

Score each of the following as +1 (improving), 0 (stable), or −1 (deteriorating), then apply a thesis relevance weight:

```
THESIS-WEIGHTED BUSINESS TRAJECTORY SCORECARD

Thesis: "[Your 1-3 sentence falsifiable thesis — from Stage 1]"

WEIGHT GUIDE:
  2x   = Thesis-core: directly tests the central claim of why you own this stock — MAX 1 metric
  1.5x = Thesis-supporting: contextualizes or reinforces the thesis — MAX 2 metrics
  1x   = General health: important for any business, but not thesis-specific — remaining metrics

Assign weights ONCE when you establish the position, not quarterly.
Changing weights each quarter defeats the purpose — it lets you
rationalize away deterioration by downgrading the metric that's failing.

                                          Score  Weight  Weighted
Revenue growth rate vs. prior quarter:    ___    ___x    ___
Gross margin vs. year-ago quarter:        ___    ___x    ___
FCF conversion vs. trailing average:      ___    ___x    ___
DSO trend:                                ___    ___x    ___
Your "one metric that matters most":      ___    ___x    ___
ROIC vs. trailing average:                ___    ___x    ___
                                Weighted Total:  ___
                              Max Possible (+1 × all weights): ___
                              Normalized Score (Total / Max):  ___%

INTERPRETATION (use the normalized percentage):
  Above 60%:   Business clearly improving
  20% to 60%:  Mixed / stable
  Below 20%:   Deterioration — review thesis

WHY WEIGHTED: An equal-weighted scorecard can mask thesis-critical
deterioration behind unrelated strength. If your thesis depends on
net deposit growth and that metric is failing, but five other general
health metrics score +1, the equal-weighted total reads "improving"
while your actual reason for owning the stock is eroding. The 2x
weight on thesis-core metrics ensures deterioration in what matters
most cannot be drowned out — while the 2x cap (not 3x) prevents
any single metric from having veto power over the entire scorecard.
The thesis kill criteria system handles the veto scenario separately.

EXAMPLE (Robinhood — HOOD):
  Thesis: "HOOD is evolving from trading platform to diversified
  financial super app capturing the next generation's wallet,
  compounding platform assets and revenue streams."

                                          Score  Weight  Weighted
  Revenue growth rate:                    +1     1.5x    +1.5
  GAAP net margin:                        +1     1x      +1
  Adj EBITDA margin:                      +1     1x      +1
  SBC as % of revenue:                    +1     1x      +1
  Net deposit growth (ONE METRIC):        −1     2x      −2
  Incremental EBITDA margin:               0     1.5x     0
                                Weighted Total:  +2.5
                                   Max Possible: +8
                              Normalized Score:  31% → Mixed / stable

  Note: The equal-weighted version scores +3/+6 = 50%, comfortably
  mid-range in the "mixed / stable" band. The thesis-weighted version
  drops to 31% — still technically stable, but dangerously close to
  the 20% deterioration threshold. That 19-point gap is the signal:
  the thesis-core metric (net deposits at 2x) is deteriorating, and
  the general health metrics scoring +1 are masking how close this
  position is to a thesis review trigger.
```

**"Thesis intact" vs. "thesis challenged" — how to determine which:**

Your thesis should be a 1-3 sentence statement of *why* you own this company. Not "it's a good company" — a specific, falsifiable claim about the business. For example:

- "I own KLAC because semiconductor process control is a structural duopoly and AI-driven advanced packaging increases their TAM by 30%+"
- "I own VEEV because FDA-validated cloud systems have 15+ year replacement cycles and no AI-native competitor can replicate their regulatory moat within 5 years"
- "I own PLTR because their government segment is a regulatory fortress and AIP is converting their commercial segment from services to platform"

For each quarterly report, ask: **Did anything in this report challenge the specific claim in my thesis?**

| Type of Challenge | Example | Response |
|-------------------|---------|----------|
| Competitive | A new entrant won a major deal from your company's customer | Investigate. One deal isn't a pattern, but note it and track. |
| Structural | A regulatory change removes a barrier to entry | Serious. Reassess the moat. This may be a thesis-ending event. |
| Execution | Management missed their own guidance by a wide margin | Reduces management credibility score. One miss is noise; two consecutive misses are a pattern. |
| Financial | ROIC declining for 3+ consecutive quarters | The compounding engine is eroding. Investigate whether it's temporary (investment cycle) or structural (competition). |
| Valuation | Stock price rose 80% while fundamentals improved 15% | Not a thesis challenge per se, but the risk/reward has shifted. Consider trimming to a smaller position, not selling entirely. |

**The Hardest Decision: When to Sell**

Most retail investors hold losers too long and sell winners too early. Two frameworks to combat this:

*The "Would I Buy It Today?" Test:*

If you didn't already own this stock, and you saw it at today's price with today's fundamentals, would you buy it? If the honest answer is "no," that's useful information. It doesn't mean you should sell immediately (tax considerations, conviction level, and portfolio construction all matter), but it should prompt you to articulate *why* you're still holding.

*The "Thesis Kill Criteria" Framework:*

When you establish a position, write down the 2-3 things that would *kill* the thesis. Not "the stock goes down" — specific, observable business developments:

```
THESIS KILL CRITERIA for [Company]
  1. If [specific metric] deteriorates to [threshold] for 2+ consecutive quarters
  2. If [specific competitive threat] materializes as evidenced by [observable signal]
  3. If management [specific credibility failure] occurs

  If ANY of these triggers fires, I will [specific action: trim by X%, exit entirely, etc.]
```

Writing these down in advance is essential because in the moment of deterioration, you will rationalize holding. The pre-commitment removes the emotional component.

## Putting It All Together: The Quarterly Cadence

### The Routine

For each position in your portfolio, the quarterly earnings cycle should look like this:

| Timing | Activity | Time Required |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| T−2 days | Pre-earnings baseline: fill in expectations sheet | 15 min |
| T+0 (release day) | First pass: scan release, check 5 headline items + quality checks | 20 min |
| T+0 or T+1 | Earnings call: read transcript with language diff focus | 30-45 min |
| T+1 to T+3 | Deep analysis: compute Tier 1 + Tier 2 + Tier 3 metrics, update scorecards | 30-45 min |
| T+3 to T+5 | Decision: update the 2×2 matrix, take action if warranted | 15 min |
| **Total per position** | | **~2 hours** |

For a concentrated portfolio of 8-12 positions, that's 16-24 hours per quarter spread across 6 weeks of earnings season. That's roughly 4-6 hours per week during the peak weeks — meaningful but manageable.

### What You Can Skip

Not everything the professionals do is relevant to your mandate. Explicitly deprioritize:

- **Estimate precision.** You don't need to predict EPS to the penny. You need to know if the business is getting better or worse. An analyst who spends 4 hours refining their gross margin estimate from 64.2% to 64.4% is serving a different purpose than you are.
- **Options-implied expectations.** Unless you're trading options, the implied move is irrelevant to your hold/buy/sell decision.
- **Intra-quarter channel checks.** You don't have access to expert networks or proprietary data sources. That's fine — your edge is time horizon, not information advantage.
- **Day-of price action analysis.** Whether the stock is up or down 5% after hours is noise for a mid-to-long-term holder. The market's initial reaction is frequently wrong and reverses within days. Focus on the fundamentals, not the tape.
- **Every sell-side note.** Read 1-2 high-quality notes from the top analysts in the coverage group (if available) to calibrate your view. Don't read 15 notes that all say the same thing with different formatting.

### What You Should Never Skip

- **The pre-earnings expectations sheet.** If you don't know what you expect, you can't identify surprises. This is the single most important habit.
- **DSO and FCF conversion.** These two metrics catch earnings quality deterioration earlier than any other signal. They take 60 seconds to calculate and they are the most information-dense numbers in the entire filing.
- **The management credibility tracker.** Compounding works in both directions. A management team that consistently underpromises and overdelivers is compounding trust. A management team that consistently overpromises and underdelivers is compounding your losses. Track it.
- **The thesis kill criteria check.** Every quarter, explicitly ask: have any of my kill criteria been triggered? If yes, act. If no, hold with conviction.

## Appendix: Quick-Reference Ratio Cheat Sheet

| Ratio | Formula | How to Pull the Inputs | Threshold for Concern |
|-------|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| DSO | (AR / Revenue) × 91 | Balance sheet AR ÷ Income statement revenue | Up 5+ days vs. year-ago quarter |
| FCF Conversion | (OCF − CapEx) / Net Income | Cash flow statement | Below 80% for capital-light; below 50% for capital-heavy |
| SBC % Revenue | SBC / Revenue | Cash flow statement (operating add-back) | Above 15% for tech; any sustained increase |
| Gross Margin | Gross Profit / Revenue | Income statement | Down 100+ bps vs. trailing 4Q average |
| Incremental Margin | ΔOperating Income / ΔRevenue | Income statement (QoQ or YoY) | Below gross margin = negative operating leverage |
| ROIC | NOPAT / Avg Invested Capital | Income statement + balance sheet | Below 12% and declining |
| Net Debt / EBITDA | (Total Debt − Cash) / Trailing EBITDA | Balance sheet + income statement | Above 3x for most industries |
| Deferred Rev Growth | (Current Deferred Rev / Prior Year Deferred Rev) − 1 | Balance sheet | Growing slower than revenue |
| Net Dilution | (Diluted Shares Now / Diluted Shares 1Y Ago) − 1 | EPS footnote in income statement | Positive (shares increasing despite buybacks) |
| Accrual Ratio | (Net Income − OCF) / Avg Total Assets | Income statement + cash flow + balance sheet | Positive and rising over 3+ quarters |
| Rule of 40 | Revenue Growth % + FCF Margin % | Income statement + cash flow statement | Below 30 for SaaS |

## Appendix: The 15-Minute Quarterly Minimum

If you're short on time and can only do the absolute minimum for a position, do exactly this — nothing more, nothing less:

1. **Check your "one metric that matters most."** Did it improve, hold steady, or deteriorate? (2 min)
2. **Calculate DSO and compare to the year-ago quarter.** (2 min)
3. **Calculate FCF conversion.** (2 min)
4. **Read the guidance section of the press release.** Raised, maintained, or lowered? (3 min)
5. **Search the earnings call transcript for your thesis keywords.** (3 min)
6. **Ask: have any of my thesis kill criteria been triggered?** (1 min)
7. **Update the decision: buy, hold, or sell.** (2 min)

If all signals are green, hold with confidence and do the full analysis when you have time. If any signal is yellow or red, prioritize the full analysis before the next trading session.
